A1: Intro to Digital Typography

Points: 30

Critique: 2 Groups

Many websites and on-screen applications are made up of reusable components of text, images and user interface elements (i.e. buttons). These components often need to be flexible, since there might be a lot of variation in the actual content they display. For instance, think about a Facebook post or an article summary on a news website.

Effective digital design needs to be scannable and clearly direct the user to the content most relevant to them. Often, for business purposes, it needs to drive the user toward a specific action.

Examples:

  • Compstak
  • Ionic Framework
  • BackBlaze
  • Heroku
  • Craft CMS

Objectives

  • Practice studying existing websites for effective methods and patterns of layout, design and user interface
  • Introduction to designing user-interfaces/web layouts in Photoshop/Illustrator
  • Introduction to designing typographic hierarchies for the screen
  • Introduction to considerations of screen size in your designs

Step 1

Screenshot 10 examples of calls to action, highlights, call-outs, or other “components” in web sites (from a curated source, such as thebestdesigns.com, onepagelove.com)

Step 2

Choose 3 of them, with instructor guidance, and do a 'study' of them: recreate each of them using Photoshop or Illustrator. You can substitute fonts when necessary.

Step 3

Design a series of lorem ipsum graphics (components) using the Open Sans typeface: which you can download from Font Squirrel

The assumption of these graphics is that they will be news article summaries on a general audience website. Specific content will vary.

Graphic set 1:

  • White background
  • Black and gray text
  • Headline
  • Short copy block (around 2–3 sentences)
  • A light gray shape to indicate a photo or icon
  • Presented at:
    • 960px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 640px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 360px wide ✕ 400px tall

Graphic 2:

  • White background
  • Black and gray text
  • Headline
  • Short copy block
  • Accessory text (2–8 words), such as an author name, date, sub headline, etc.
  • 1 hairline rule
  • A light gray shape to indicate a photo or icon
  • Presented at:
    • 960px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 640px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 360px wide ✕ 400px tall

Graphic 3:

  • White background
  • Black and gray text, plus 1 color
  • Headline
  • Short copy block
  • Accessory text ( 2–8 words)
  • 1 hairline or thick rule
  • A light gray shape to indicate a photo or icon
  • Presented at:
    • 960px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 640px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 360px wide ✕ 400px tall

Graphic 4:

  • White background
  • Black and gray text, plus 1 color
  • Headline
  • Short copy block
  • Accessory text (2–8 words)
  • Accessory text 2 (should have a different hierarchy level than the first accessory text)
  • 1 hairline or thick rule is optional
  • A light gray shape to indicate a photo or icon
  • Presented at:
    • 960px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 640px wide ✕ 400px tall
    • 360px wide ✕ 400px tall

Each size change in a graphic set should be a 'reflowing' of the design. In other words, the design should be similar, with slight modifications so that it fits in the alternative sizes.

Presentation

  1. Add your final graphics to your InVision board.
  2. Make a section for the 3 studies called Assignment 1, Study 1.
  3. Drag the graphics into this section, along with their original screenshots. Set them each to take up half the screen.
  4. Repeat for your other 2 studies.
  5. Make a section called Assignment 1, Graphic 1, above the studies.
  6. Drag the graphics from Graphic Set 1 into this section
  7. Set the large graphic size 4 in Invision
  8. Set the medium graphic to size 2
  9. Set the small graphic to size 1 (default)
  10. Do this for each of your graphic sets.

In InVision, make sure your Assignment and graphics flow so that the newest items are at the top. Assignment 1, Study 1 should be the last section of your page.
